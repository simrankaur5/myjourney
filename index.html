About Myself And My Work Experiences

My name is Simran Kaur. I was born in the month of December in Mwanza which is a city in Tanzania, East Africa. I am currently studying for a degree in Computer Science at Goldsmiths, University Of London.

Work Experience
Working life in Tanzania is considered as an adult's task. Students never look for a job unless they are graduated or if they do not wish to continue studying. On the other hand, looking for jobs in Mwanza was difficult as it was a small city and it was not highly developed economically. However when I moved to London I was amazed by the job opportunities students had while studying to enhance their experience in the practical and working world. Many students work part time whilst studying and students in year 10 get an opportunity to do work experience for 4 weeks. This strengthens their CV for future employment.

When I was in year 10, I had the opportunity to do work experience. I worked as a trainee receptionist at Gilroy Court Hotel, Croydon. I found this as a useful experience because it was my first time ever in the working world and I had gained invaluable skills which included and were not limited to communication, time management and problem solving skills. I then experienced working at Battersea Pharmacy whilst studying in year 13. This again was a wonderful opportunity for me to enhance the skills I had acquired and learn new skills.

In January 2017, I got my first job at Gatwick Airport as a sales assistant. I was very pleased and also excited to start this new job. I have since had many different experiences as I get to meet people from around the world who belong to different cultures, religions and ethnicities everytime I go to work.

School Differences

Tanzania

What was the admissions procedure for secondary school?

I had to take a Maths and English exam and the results were compared to other students taking the same test. The schools choose the top 50 students and the classes are divided into 2 with 25 students each.

How strict were teachers and how disciplined were their students?

In Tanzania failing an exam or even a class test was taken into account which meant being punished with sticks! If a student scored below 30% it was considered as failing and so the student would be disciplined with a punishement of sticks on the palm of their hand! This was not just to do with failing exams or tests but making noise in the class or disrespecting a teacher was taken into consideration. This clearly was done so that students studied hard and also stayed disciplined
I have experienced being punished with sticks however I was a disciplined student and liked by my teachers. I experienced this form of punishment on two instances, once when the whole class was making noise and another time was when I scored low marks in a Maths test. It was painful but I feel it did help me study hard for future class tests however getting beaten because of the class making noise was never justified.

Techniques used in teaching

The teachers wrote on blackboards using chalks and the students would copy down everything written on the blackboards. There were textbooks but those were used only for revising during exams and for questions given for homework.

Transport to school

I used to go to school by a private bus that was only for my school. Sometimes the bus would break down or the driver was ill so I had to go to school or come back home walking which was not difficult as my house was not far from the school and as the weather was always pleasant I enjoyed walking. There was other ways of getting to school such as the public buses or by car or by bicycle.

Sport activities

We had physical activities as part of the school curriculum. We always used to have outdoor sports because it was always warm unless if it rained the lesson would be cancelled. We played competing games where all the class could take part. Some of the games were swimming, football, cricket, a variety of racing games and dodge ball.

Social Life at school

At primary school when it was your birthday you were allowed to wear clothes of your own choice rather than the school uniform to make your day even more special. Also you would take a cake and cut it with your classmates. This was a good way of interacting with your teachers and classmates and it also made you feel special.

London

My first day at school

I had a negative experience when I went to school for the first time in London. I did not fit in as I felt I was different from the others because of the different school environment in Tanzania compared to London. I felt insecure because some students were laughing at me whereas many students surrounded me and it made me feel like I was the centre of attention but not in a positive way.

Techniques used in teaching

The teachers either write on whiteboards using whiteboard markers or they use smartboards which is the latest technology. It connects to the computer and the teacher can display presentation slides of notes for students to copy down or to follow along as the topic is explained.

Transport to school

I went to school by public transport which is the common and most convenient way of transport in London. I had to take two buses to get to school. I found it very difficult for the first few months as I had never been on public transport since I moved to London. The buses were always packed with school children and sometimes the people on the buses were quite rude and unpolite as they would push into me or try to squeeze in even though there was no space! This experience on the public transport did put me off but I got accustomed to the morning and evening chaos on the buses after a while.

Sport activities

We had physical activities as part of the school curriculum similar to Tanzania. However unlike Tanzania, we had indoor sports because of the unpredictable weather in London. We played competitive games where all the class could take part. Some of the games were basketball, netball, badminton, table tennis and dodge ball. During summer we did have outdoor activities.

Difference In Lifestyle

Compared to Tanzania, London is known for its health services i.e. NHS. In Tanzania getting any medical advice or going to the doctor was quite expensive regardless of your age. There were limited resources, doctors and hospitals and critically ill patients would have to be transferred to hospitals outside the region and sometimes country. When I moved to London I was impressed by the health care citizens recieve including 24 hours access to emergency services. Click on the image for more information.

In Tanzania having neighbours was uncommon as the houses were big and people built their houses at a distance from each other however there were flats as well. I lived and grew up in a house surrounded by a community so since my childhood I have learnt to communicate and interact with my neighbours and community. When I moved to London, I noticed that almost everyone is busy with their own lives and even though everyone has neighbours it is rare to see each other or even know one another but in Tanzania I would see my neighbours on a regular basis. This was a bit odd for me when I came to London but I understand this behaviour now since I too have become so busy and captured in the fast pace of life.
